⚠️ The "100% Behavioural Compliance" Trap

The council's financial model assumes that 100% of affected families will accept losing transport and fund their own travel to their preferred catchment school.

If parents instead enroll their children in their new "nearest" school to secure free transport, the council must commission new, un-optimised routes, driving up costs.
⏳ The Cohort Lag Paradox

Because the policy applies only to new intake pupils, large capacity buses must continue running the same routes at 100% contract costs to service grandfathered cohorts.

The council's model falsely reports immediate savings on routes where vehicle contract costs remain entirely fixed for years.

How This Model Works & Why It Is Accurate

Overview

This model is an independent financial audit tool designed to evaluate the true fiscal impact of changes to school transport policies. Rather than assuming instant savings and 100% compliance, it models the actual operational transitions, grandfathering clauses, and appeals funnels that occur when transport eligibility rules are changed.

How It Works

  • Cohort-Based Transport Costs: Since policy changes typically apply only to new intakes, older cohorts continue to run under existing arrangements. The model calculates alternative vehicle costs year-by-year, summing the active student cohorts in the system, each eroding by a set rate (attrition) over their 5-year school career.
  • Chained Appeal Progression: Disputing transport decisions escalates through three distinct, chained stages: Stage 1 (Internal Review), Stage 2 (Independent Panel), and Stage 3 (Ombudsman). The model chains these rates (e.g., Stage 2 is a percentage of Stage 1, not of the total population) and applies the selected unit costs to each dispute.
  • Maturity-Phased Savings: Claimed savings do not happen instantly. The model phases in the council's savings claim over 8 years, maturing to the full claimed annual amount in Year 8 based on the council's implementation steps (starting at 19.7% in Year 1 and reaching 100% in Year 8).
  • Ongoing Administration: Captures the yearly software, mapping, system update, and liaison overheads required to administer a fractured transport network.

Why It Is Accurate

  • Reflects Fixed Cost Realities: Large school buses cannot be cancelled immediately if even a few grandfathered students remain on the route. Cohort modeling prevents the model from claiming "day one" bus route savings.
  • Accurate Appellate Chaining: By modeling appeals as a funnel (Stage 2 funnels from Stage 1; Ombudsman funnels from Stage 2), the model reflects actual administrative volumes and legal escalation rates.
  • Audited Cost Parameters: Default unit costs and dispute rates are derived from audited local government accounts and historic appeals volumes, ensuring realistic baseline projections.

Geographic Realities: Volume-Based Vehicle Scaling

Why 50 Zones?

This number directly maps to the 50+ distinct rural primary school feeder clusters across North Yorkshire. The council's "nearest school" shift impacts specific village lines and primary communities, not entire towns evenly. Dividing the baseline across 50 feeder clusters yields roughly 6.8 children per community per year, ensuring the model assumes children are scattered across 50 separate micro-geographies rather than allowing impossible county-wide routing efficiencies.

The Isolation Rate

Recognises that a portion of deeply rural pupils live down isolated lanes or single-track roads. They can never be grouped into a larger route without causing unacceptable transit times, leaving them permanently reliant on individual taxi contracts (capacity of 3).

The Minibus Threshold

Models how costs behave in the real world. In Years 1–2, low student density forces high reliance on individual taxis. Only when the 5-year cohort stack builds up over time do village clusters reach a critical mass (the threshold) that allows the council to upgrade to a consolidated minibus (capacity of 16, costing 1.5x of a standard taxi contract).

The Coach Threshold

Models the long-term saturation of the policy. In Years 5–8, as the rolling 5-year cohort stack completely fills up, certain high-density primary feeder zones will hit critical mass (17+ active pupils). At this point, the model dynamically rewards the council by consolidating the minibus route back into a full-capacity coach contract, accurately reflecting the long-term stabilization of a fully embedded transport network.
